relationship formation/introductions: interactions that initiate contact and establish basic social footing (names, roles, context) to open the door to further connection.

relationship formation/getting-to-know-you: low-stakes exchanges used to learn someone’s background, personality, and general vibe while keeping risk and vulnerability limited.

relationship formation/light disclosure: sharing small personal details (preferences, harmless opinions, daily life) to invite reciprocity and test safety.

relationship formation/mutual interests: identifying shared topics, hobbies, goals, or tastes to create easy conversation loops and a sense of similarity.

relationship formation/boundary testing: subtle probes of limits such as humor, teasing, questions, topics etc. to see what’s acceptable and how the other person responds.

relationship maintenance/routine check-ins: short interactions that reaffirm connection and continuity such as messages and calls etc without needing major content.

relationship maintenance/shared rituals: repeated activities that anchor the relationship such as weekly meals, traditions or inside routines and create stability.

relationship maintenance/availability signalling: behaviors that show you’re reliably there such as timely replies, remembering details or showing up to keep the bond secure.

relationship maintenance/low-stakes bonding: casual talk and small shared moments that maintain warmth without heavy emotional work.

relationship maintenance/relationship housekeeping: practical conversations that keep the relationship functioning such as plans, expectations, small adjustments or coordinating life.

relationship deepening/vulnerability sharing: revealing meaningful feelings, fears, or hopes to invite deeper trust and emotional closeness.

relationship deepening/value alignment: discussing beliefs and priorities to see whether you “match” at a deeper level and can build shared meaning.

relationship deepening/reciprocal self-disclosure: a back-and-forth exchange of personal information that gradually increases intimacy through balanced openness.

relationship deepening/commitment talk: conversations that clarify exclusivity, loyalty, future intentions, and what we are to each other.

relationship deepening/creating shared identity: building a shared identity through private language, joint stories, and shared goals that distinguish the relationship.

emotional support/active listening: attentive presence focused on understanding the speaker’s experience without rushing to fix or judge.

emotional support/validation: communicating that feelings make sense given the situation, reducing shame and defensiveness.

emotional support/reassurance: offering safety cues such as care, acceptance and stability to reduce anxiety and catastrophic thinking.

emotional support/encouragement: strength-focused support that boosts confidence and motivation without dismissing difficulty.

emotional support/practical help as care: Tangible assistance (tasks, resources, problem handling) offered primarily to reduce burden and signal support.

co-regulation/de-escalation: techniques to lower emotional intensity in the moment such as slowing pace, soft tone, or grounding language to prevent spirals.

co-regulation/grounding and calming: helping someone return to baseline through steady presence, breathing cues, and predictable reassurance.

co-regulation/energizing and activation: raising morale or momentum when someone is stuck or low-energy through enthusiasm, structure, and gentle pushes.

co-regulation/containment: creating a safe emotional container including time limits, focus and boundaries so strong feelings are expressed without overwhelming either person.

co-regulation/affect matching: mildly mirroring tone and emotion to signal understanding, then gradually guiding toward calmer states.

information sharing/status updates: brief factual updates about what is happening or changed, primarily to keep others informed.

information sharing/announcements: one-to-many or formal updates that communicate decisions, events, or changes with clear expectations.

information sharing/knowledge transfer: explaining facts, procedures, or background so someone can understand or act correctly.

information sharing/clarification: short exchanges that reduce confusion by defining terms, correcting misunderstandings, or specifying details.

information sharing/resource sharing: providing links, documents, contacts, or references to enable learning or action.

sensemaking/discussion to understand: open-ended talk aimed at forming a clearer picture rather than proving a point or winning.

sensemaking/cause-and-effect interpretation: exploring why something happened, what factors mattered, and what patterns might explain it.

sensemaking/scenario planning: considering plausible future outcomes and what signals would indicate each path.

sensemaking/risk appraisal: estimating likelihood and impact of threats or opportunities to guide caution or action.

sensemaking/debate-for-learning: structured disagreement used to test ideas, uncover assumptions, and refine understanding without personal attack.

coordination/scheduling: aligning times, deadlines, and availability so actions happen smoothly.

coordination/role allocation: assigning responsibilities and ownership so everyone knows who does what.

coordination/process alignment: agreeing on how work will be done including steps, tools, and communication channels to reduce friction.

coordination/progress tracking: checking status, updating blockers, and adjusting plans to stay on course.

coordination/hand-offs: transferring tasks or information across people with enough clarity to avoid drops and duplication.

joint problem-solving/problem definition: clarifying what the real problem is, what constraints exist, and what is output looks like.

joint problem-solving/brainstorming options: Generating multiple possible solutions without immediately judging them.

joint problem-solving/root-cause analysis: identifying underlying drivers rather than treating symptoms so fixes actually stick.

joint problem-solving/testing and iteration: trying solutions, observing results, and refining based on feedback.

joint problem-solving/tradeoff evaluation: comparing options by cost, risk, time, and impact to pick the most workable approach.

decision-making/option narrowing: reducing many possibilities into a short list using agreed criteria.

decision-making/choice and closure: selecting a direction and explicitly ending indecision so action can start.

decision-making/buy-in check: ensuring stakeholders genuinely accept the decision or can live with it to prevent sabotage or drift.

decision-making/commitment setting: converting decisions into concrete commitments such as who, what, when and accountability.

decision-making/review and adjustment: revisiting decisions after new information to confirm, modify, or reverse responsibly.

persuasion/evidence-based argument: using facts, logic, and examples to shift beliefs or preferences.

persuasion/framing and narrative: presenting an issue through a particular lens or story to make one interpretation feel more compelling.

persuasion/emotional appeal: influencing by engaging values, fear, hope, pride, or empathy to motivate change.

persuasion/social proof and credibility: using status, expertise, endorsements, or group norms to increase acceptance.

persuasion/call to action: moving from agreement to behavior by making the next step easy, specific, and timely.

negotiation/interest discovery: identifying what each side truly wants beneath stated positions to find workable overlaps.

negotiation/proposal making: offering terms that bundle benefits and costs in a way the other side might accept.

negotiation/counteroffers: iteratively adjusting terms to move toward a mutually acceptable deal.

negotiation/concession trading: exchanging like I’ll give X if you give Y to balance value and fairness.

negotiation/agreement formalization: making the final deal explicit such as scope, timelines, responsibilities, contingencies to prevent later conflict.

boundary-setting/saying no: declining requests while clearly stating limits without excessive justification or hostility.

boundary-setting/expectation setting: defining what you can and cannot do, what you need, and what others can expect going forward.

boundary-setting/consent and respect checks: ensuring interaction remains voluntary and respectful, especially around sensitive topics or intimacy.

boundary-setting/consequence communication: explaining what will happen if limits are violated to make boundaries enforceable.

boundary-setting/relationship-preserving firmness: maintaining warmth while staying clear and steady so boundaries don’t become fights.

conflict expression/competition for resources: fighting over scarce things (time, money, recognition, control) where one side’s gain feels like another’s loss.

conflict expression/value or identity clash: disagreement tied to moral beliefs or identity, where compromise can feel like betrayal.

conflict expression/escalation behaviors: raising intensity through sarcasm, interruptions, threats, public shaming, or absolutist language.

de-escalation/slowdown and pause: interrupting the escalation cycle by stopping, taking breaks, and lowering tempo before continuing.

de-escalation/reflecting and paraphrasing: demonstrating understanding of the other side’s point to reduce the need for repetition and intensity.

de-escalation/reframing the issue: shifting from personal attack to shared problem, or from who’s right to what works.

de-escalation/needs clarification: identifying underlying needs and fears driving the conflict to create pathways beyond positions.

de-escalation/structured dialogue: using rules such as turn-taking, time limits, specific questions to keep discussion constructive.

repair/making amends: concrete actions that restore value or safety such as restitution, corrections, supportive follow-through.

caretaking/developmental guidance: shaping habits, values, and skills through coaching, structure, and consistent limits.
