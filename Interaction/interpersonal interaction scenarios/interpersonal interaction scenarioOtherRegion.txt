matter: Athens tries to force Melos to submit and pay tribute, then besieges the island when it refuses.
actors: Athens, imperial power issuing the ultimatum and leading the expedition; Cleomedes and Tisias, commanders of the Athenian force; Melian leadership, negotiating and deciding resistance; Sparta, the hoped-for Melian protector and deterrent.
result: After negotiations fail, Athens tightens the siege, Melos surrenders, and Athens executes the adult men, enslaves the women and children, and plants an Athenian settlement.

description: Melos tried to remain outside Athens’ empire during the wider war, but Athens judged that letting a conspicuously independent island stay neutral would signal weakness to subjects and allies. In 416 BC an Athenian expedition arrived, encamped, and opened with private negotiations, producing the famous confrontation in which Athens argues that power decides outcomes and that submission is the only rational path to survival, while the Melians appeal to neutrality, justice, reputation, and the possibility that Sparta or fortune might intervene. Athens refuses to treat moral claims as decisive and presses a final choice between tribute and safety or resistance and destruction. The Melians choose resistance, so Athens moves from argument to method, investing the city, cutting off supplies, and sustaining a siege until surrender becomes unavoidable. When the city falls, Athens turns the outcome into a warning to others, killing the men, enslaving the rest, and resettling the island, converting a political choice about independence into an imperial object lesson.

The Peloponnesian fleet initially pushes the Athenians back, but the intensity of the pursuit stretches formations and creates pockets of vulnerability. In that moment, Athenian commanders shift from survival to exploitation, concentrating force where the enemy line is least coherent and using a local breakthrough to unravel the larger engagement. The victory’s meaning exceeds its raw ship losses, because it arrives when Athens is politically unstable and materially strained, and a defeat could have severed its lifeline to grain and allies. Thucydides presents it as a pivot of psychological momentum, showing how in protracted war, belief in the possibility of survival can be as strategic as territory.
