community: a group of people who share common interests, goals, or characteristics,   connecting and interacting with each other and forming certain ties among them. Communities can take various forms of relationships and structures, ranging from local neighborhoods to virtual networks. Communities often have established norms, values, and cultural practices that guide behavior and interactions among members. Not always, a community is often organized, run or supported by an organization. For example, neighborhood communities are supported by the government; volunteer communities are organized by NGO, non-profit organization or church; trade associations are supported by companies; or learner communities are hosted by schools etc.

community components: leaders - lead and direct the community, who is always a member of the community, and must have vision, passion, trust and capabilities; organizers - design, organize and implement processes and activities, who can be members or non-members, and must have passion and capabilities; members - participate, support, engage etc. , who must have sense of belonging and feel being accepted, respected and supported; sense of belonging - varies in degree along a continuum, which is influenced by a few factors including a member’s motivation, the opportunities of interaction, the community’s reaction and the member’s perception, the match of the member and the community in terms of personalities, capabilities, belief, identity etc; sense of ownership - the degree of members to take the initiatives with sense of being owners in the community; supporting organizations - organizations that support, host, fund, organize or run the communities such as NGO, companies, church, government etc.; purpose - the reason for the community to exist; practices - activities, recurring behaviors, norms and culture; collaboration - cohesiveness and alignment among community members; trust - authenticity and capabilities of community members; strength of bond and intentionality - the strength of the interests and commitment on a common topic or goal; degree of institutionalization - the degree of centralized control and influence, and the degree of openness to public; effectiveness - the degree of achieving the purpose and goal; evolvement - the direction in which the community is changing to.

community type/geographic or space-based community: a community formed by people who live in the same physical area or region and interact with each other based on their shared location. This type of community is defined by spatial proximity and is characterized by the geographic location where its members reside. Community members interaction is facilitated by physical closeness, which often includes face-to-face encounters and local events. Members may share concerns related to local issues, such as infrastructure, safety, or community events. They often share access to local resources, such as schools, parks, and public services. Geographic communities often have shared cultural practices, traditions, or customs specific to their location. Examples include neighborhoods, towns and cities, villages etc.

community type/professional community: a community formed by a group of individuals who share a common profession or occupation and engage with each other based on their professional interests and goals. These communities provide a platform for networking, collaboration, and development within a specific field or industry, such as medicine, engineering, education, or business etc.. It provides opportunities for members to connect with peers, mentors, and potential employers or collaborators, and also facilitates collaboration on projects, research, or initiatives within the field. It offers resources for ongoing education and skill development, such as workshops, seminars, or training sessions, and information and support for obtaining professional certifications or accreditations. Members share industry news, trends, research findings, and best practices. It reinforces a professional identity and sense of belonging within the field. It can advocate for the interests and needs of the profession, influencing policies and standards.

community type/interest-based community: formed by people who share a common hobby, interest or passion, such as a sport activity, gaming, photography, gardening or volunteering etc.. Members interact with each other through discussions, events, or activities related to their shared interest. They may collaborate on projects or participate in group activities centered around their interest. Members share knowledge, tips, and advice related to their interest. It provides a network of like-minded individuals who can provide advice, feedback, and companionship. It may involve sharing resources or tools related to the interest.

community type/ethnic community: formed by a group of people who share a common ethnic background, which includes shared cultural practices, language, heritage, traditions, and often a common ancestry. Ethnic communities are characterized by their cultural identity and are often bound together by their ethnic origins and shared historical experiences. Some examples are Latino Community, Jewish Community, Chinese American Community etc.

community type/religious community: formed by a group of individuals who share a common religious faith, practice, or spiritual belief system. Members of a religious community come together to worship, support one another, and live according to shared religious values and teachings. These communities can vary greatly in size, structure, and practice, depending on the religion and its traditions. Some examples are: Buddhist community, Christianity community, Muslim community etc.

community type/learning community: formed by people who learn together with teachers or tutors. The learning community strongly depends on the tutor, who guides his students in a learning process linked to the disciplinary or transdisciplinary objectives of the curriculum or studies programme. The activity of the community is according to the level of development of the learners and to the institutional context of belonging. There are three types of learning communities. Academic learning community - schools, colleagues, universities etc.. Professional learning community - professionals within a particular field or organization who come together to enhance their professional skills, share best practices, and solve common challenges. Interest-based learning community - these communities are formed around hobbies, passions, or interests. 

community type/volunteers community: formed by a group of individuals who come together to offer their time, skills, and efforts to support causes, organizations, or projects without monetary compensation. These communities are driven by a shared commitment to making a positive impact in their communities or in broader societal contexts. A volunteer community has a concrete organization structure, clear roles and responsibilities for volunteers to follow and communicate their vision and mission clearly. It places the right people on the right track and trains them with the right skills. It also celebrates achievements and recognition. Volunteers will experience a strong sense of achievement when their individual talents and skills are matched with the volunteer assignment. Recognition is a need for many volunteers. Individuals with families seek out opportunities to volunteer which will increase family time together. Volunteer management includes: set vision and policies, define needs and identify risks and mitigation, recruit volunteers, onboard and train volunteers, support and recognize volunteers, review and evaluate. Volunteers need to feel that they are learning and growing from their volunteer experience. Volunteer pathways include new volunteer, specialist - experienced volunteers dedicated to a specific programme leveraging their expertise or specialized skills to provide enhanced support to staff in implementing  programmes, creator - design new programmes, events, or training by supporting staff individually or as part of a committee, leader - play a key role in enhancing volunteer engagement by offering fellow volunteers guidance, support, and mentorship.


community type/identity-based community: formed by a group of individuals who come together based on a shared aspect of their identity. This could be related to various factors such as gender, sexual orientation, socioeconomic status, education, work or other aspects that contribute to a person’s sense of self and belonging. It celebrates and preserves cultural practices, traditions, and customs associated with their identity, and organizes events, gatherings, or activities that reinforce the shared aspect of identity and build community cohesion. It may provide emotional and practical support for issues and challenges related to their shared identity. It may also engage in advocacy or activism to address issues and promote rights related to their identity. Some examples are LQGBT group, alumni club, colleagues group etc.

community type/virtual community: formed by a group of individuals who interact and engage with each other primarily through online platforms, rather than through face-to-face interactions.

Community type/hybrid community: formed by a group of individuals who interact and engage with each other both through online platforms and face-to-face interactions.

community success factors/community members: members’ broad and strong awareness sparks interests in participation; members’ motivation; size of geographical area e.g. smaller area are more manageable; flexibility and adaptability in solving problems and accomplishing tasks; preexisting social cohesion i.e. the strength of inter-relationships among members; ability to discuss and reach consensus and cooperate; existing identifiable leadership; prior success with community building.

community success factors/community organizers: organizers understand the community with sincere commitment, a relationship of trust, sufficient organizing experience and being able to be flexible and adaptable.

community success factors/community building process: widespread participation; good system of communication; minimum competition in pursuit of goals; develop self-understanding, group identities, clarify priorities, and agree on how to achieve goals;  benefit to many members; focus on result and process concurrently; linkage with organizations outside communities; progression from simple matters to complex tasks; systematic gathering information and analysis community issues; training to get community building skills; early involvement of existing indigenous organizations; use of technical assistance; continual emergence of leaders; community control of decision making; the right mix of resources
